# Kill-case obituary

**Status:** TEMPLATE — written annually.
**Anchor:** `00-positioning.md`.

Before each year ends, write Bloom's hypothetical obituary. What killed it? What were the warning signs that didn't get acted on? This forces honest assessment instead of optimistic narrative.

## The format

Write 1-2 paragraphs in PAST TENSE about Bloom's failure. Then list the 5-10 specific decisions / non-decisions that caused it.

Honest. No "we just didn't get lucky" hand-waving.

## Patterns to watch for

When writing the kill-case, common patterns that show up in failed B2B SaaS:

- **Founder time-split.** Founder split bandwidth across N projects. Each got 1/N attention. Bloom needed N attention.
- **Pricing fear.** Undercharged early because "the customer might say no." Result: ARR insufficient to fund growth, no power to refuse bad-fit customers.
- **Feature kitchen-sink.** Said yes to every prospect's customization. Roadmap became a list of one-off requests. Brain-shape diluted.
- **Hire-too-early or too-late.** Either burned the runway pre-PMF or refused to hire post-PMF and bottlenecked at founder bandwidth.
- **Investor mismatch.** Took capital from an investor whose thesis was "AI for weddings" not "brain-shape vertical SaaS." Resulting board pressure pushed toward CRM features.
- **Compliance crisis.** A SEV1 PII leak that 2-3 customers churned over and the rest demanded enterprise contracts on hold. Round 7 + 9 + 11 audits caught real bugs; one we don't catch is the kill.
- **Single point of failure.** Anthropic outage, Supabase outage, Vercel outage that took down Bloom for > 4h on a Saturday wedding. Brand never recovers.

## How to use this doc

- **End of every year:** write the past-year entry as if Bloom died.
- **Quarterly:** re-read the most recent entry. Is the kill-vector currently active? If yes, what changed?
- **Before any major decision:** consult this list. Does the decision push toward or away from a kill vector?
- **Before any fundraise:** investors WILL ask "what kills you?" Have a credible answer. The kill-case should match the investor's expected risks closely enough that they trust your judgment.

## Anti-rule

Don't write the kill-case to comfort yourself ("we'd never do that, we're too disciplined"). Write it as if the failure already happened and you're explaining it at the wake.
