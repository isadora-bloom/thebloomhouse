# Fund-returner math vs realistic ceiling

**Status:** TEMPLATE.
**Anchor:** `00-positioning.md`.

Whether a Series A investor's required exit return is mathematically reachable from the Bloom shape, and what assumptions a "realistic" outcome makes.

## The fund-returner test

A typical Series A fund needs each portfolio company to have a credible path to returning the fund. For a $200M fund with 25 portfolio companies, each company needs to be capable of a $200M+ return on the fund's check.

If the Series A check is $5M at $25M post-money (20% ownership), the company needs to exit at $1B+ for the fund to return on this single position.

**Bloom Series A test:** can Bloom plausibly reach a $1B exit?

## At what ARR does Bloom approach $1B exit value?

Public + comparable SaaS multiples (vertical SaaS, brain-shaped, low churn):

- 2018-2021 boom: 12-20x ARR
- 2024 reset: 5-8x ARR
- 2026 conservative: 6-10x ARR

For $1B at 8x: needs $125M ARR.

For $1B at 12x (premium brain-shape multiple): needs $83M ARR.

## Path to $125M ARR

Working backwards from `01-tam-sam-som.md` and `04-b2b2c-distribution.md`:

| Year | ARR | Driver |
|---|---|---|
| Y1 (2026) | $0.3M | Rixey + 5-10 friend-of-Isadora venues |
| Y2 | $1.5M | Founder-led GTM + first multi-venue customer (small) |
| Y3 | $5M | First sales hire, Wedgewood-tier pilot |
| Y4 | $15M | Expansion-vertical pilot (B&B or corporate) live |
| Y5 | $40M | Wedgewood-tier in production + international pilot |
| Y6 | $80M | Full multi-vertical motion |
| Y7 | $125M | Mature multi-vertical + acquired complementary tech |

**This is fund-returner-shaped.** The math works IF the Wedgewood-tier customer materialises by Y3 and an expansion vertical proves out by Y4.

## Realistic ceiling math

What if Wedgewood doesn't sign? What if expansion verticals fail?

Bloom-as-a-Rixey-tier-SaaS-only at $799 ACV, capped at single-venue customers, at SAM penetration of ~5%:

| Year | ARR | Customer count |
|---|---|---|
| Y3 | $2.5M | 250 venues |
| Y5 | $8M | 800 venues |
| Y7 | $20M | 2K venues |
| Y10 | $50M | 5K venues |

**This is NOT fund-returner-shaped at typical Series A terms.** This is a $200-400M outcome at exit (8x), good for the founder, marginal for investors.

## What investors will actually price

A 2026-vintage Series A investor sees:
- Bloom shape: brain layer, vertical SaaS, defensible voice DNA moat
- Founder profile: deep operator background (Rixey)
- Customer count: < 10 at Series A
- ARR: < $1M

Pricing depends on the strength of the multi-venue + expansion-vertical narrative. Without those: $25-40M post-money is realistic for a mid-tier fund. With them: $50-80M post-money for a tier-1 fund chasing AI-vertical-SaaS.

## Honest answer

**The fund-returner outcome is reachable but requires hitting two specific milestones:**

1. **Multi-venue customer signed by Y3.** Not necessarily Wedgewood; could be a smaller roll-up. Without one, the data-network-effect moat doesn't compound and the realistic ceiling becomes the ceiling.

2. **Expansion vertical proves out by Y4.** Single-vertical wedding venues caps SOM at the wedding industry. Multi-vertical 3-5x's the SOM.

If both miss: the math forces a sale or a venture-debt-funded slower path, not a fund-returner exit.

If one hits, the other can follow.

If both miss: Bloom is a great $50M ARR business, founder gets a great outcome, fund returners can come from the OTHER 24 portfolio companies.

## Decision implications

- **For fundraise sequencing:** raise the Series A only after the first multi-venue contract is in hand or an expansion-vertical pilot is signed. Reduces investor risk; prices up.
- **For capital structure:** raise the LEAST equity that gets to that milestone. Don't sell 25% of the company before the data network effect is provable.
- **For fund-returner ALIGNMENT:** find an investor who's bought into the brain-shape thesis specifically, not generic "AI for weddings." Generic investors will push you toward CRM features at the first downturn.
