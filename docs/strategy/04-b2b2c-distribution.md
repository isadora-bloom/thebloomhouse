# B2B2C distribution model — at scale

**Status:** TEMPLATE.
**Anchor:** `00-positioning.md` (we sell the brain to venues; venues serve couples).

How Bloom's customer-acquisition model + product-distribution model evolve as ARR grows. Three checkpoints. The shape changes meaningfully at each.

## At $1M ARR — founder-led

**Customer count:** ~125-150 venues at $799 ACV blended.

**Acquisition motion:**
- Founder (Isadora) + word of mouth from Rixey
- Conferences, podcast appearances, industry-association talks
- Inbound from Bloom marketing site + Instagram
- One sales-assisted enterprise customer pulling on Wedgewood-tier features

**Couple-side distribution:** every venue customer onboards their own couples. Couple portal access is a venue-curated invite, not a Bloom-driven signup.

**B2B2C consideration:** couples never directly transact with Bloom. They use the portal because the venue gave them access. Bloom has zero CAC on couples; venues' CAC subsumes ours.

**Risks at this stage:**
- Founder bandwidth caps at ~30-40 customer touches / week
- Success means saying no to Wedgewood pilots that demand bespoke build
- Couple-side referral loops not yet built (couples don't tell their friends about Bloom; they tell them about the venue)

## At $5M ARR — first non-founder seller

**Customer count:** ~600-750 venues. Or ~125 venues + 1 multi-venue Wedgewood-scale customer.

**Acquisition motion:**
- One AE owns enterprise (Wedgewood-tier multi-venue)
- One CSM owns expansion + retention
- Founder owns pricing + new-vertical exploration
- Marketing motion shifts to content + SEO + paid (pilot)

**Couple-side distribution:** still venue-driven invite, but Bloom can now build couple-side acquisition loops:
- Couple-shareable artifacts (timeline PDF, day-of view) carry "Powered by Bloom" footer that converts at [___]% — needs measurement
- Couple-to-couple referral when newlyweds recommend their venue (venue tells them "we used Bloom" so the next couple asks for it)
- This is where B2B2C network effects kick in for the FIRST TIME

**Risks at this stage:**
- Hiring sales is hard for a brain product (sellers default to CRM pitch)
- Multi-venue customer demands custom build that pulls roadmap
- Couple-side acquisition loops fail to convert (venues turn off the footer to keep the relationship clean)

## At $20M ARR — multi-channel, multi-vertical

**Customer count:** ~2.5K venues across original wedding vertical + first expansion verticals (B&B, corporate, retreat per `12-expansion-verticals.md`).

**Acquisition motion:**
- Multiple AEs, vertical specialists
- Channel partnerships with HoneyBook / Aisle Planner (Bloom integrates IN as a brain layer rather than competing horizontally)
- International expansion underway
- Founder shifts to product + CEO; sales motion is operationalised

**Couple-side distribution:**
- "Couple network" becomes a real thing — couples have used Bloom for their own wedding and now refer the venues their friends are considering
- This becomes a measurable acquisition channel
- Possibly: a consumer-facing brand for couples ("BloomMatch") that recommends venues based on Bloom's intel

**Risks at this stage:**
- Vertical complexity stretches the brain in ways it wasn't trained for
- Channel partner dynamics (HoneyBook decides Bloom's brain is competitive, ends partnership)
- International data residency requires actual SCC compliance work (per `data-region-and-scc.md`)

## The B2B2C question that matters

**At what ARR does the couple-side become a meaningful CAC reduction channel?**

If never: Bloom is pure B2B SaaS. Pricing must support full B2B CAC.

If at $5M: couple-side referral becomes a real lever; sales motion can lean on it.

If only at $20M+: the model matures slowly. Don't bet on it for early-stage growth.

**Working assumption (until measured):** couple-side becomes a meaningful CAC reducer at $5M ARR. Plan acquisition funding accordingly. Measure the "Powered by Bloom" footer conversion rate from Day 1.
