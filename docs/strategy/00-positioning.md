# Positioning — Bloom is a brain

**Date locked:** 2026-05-08 · **Decided by:** Isadora Martin-Dye

C3 answer: Bloom is **a brain**. Not a CRM, not a marketplace, not a planning tool.

Every strategic doc downstream of this anchors here. When a feature, a hire, a marketing claim, or a partnership is on the table, the test is: does it make Bloom smarter, or does it move us toward CRM / marketplace / planning?

## What "brain" means concretely

The brain reconstructs forensic identity from every signal that touches a venue (per the constitution: "Bloom is forensic identity reconstruction"). It learns the venue's voice, the couple's network, the source attribution chain. It produces drafts, decisions, and intelligence the operator could not produce alone.

The brain does NOT:
- Replace a CRM (HoneyBook / Tripleseat / Aisle Planner do that, badly, but they do it)
- Run a marketplace (The Knot / Zola / WeddingWire do that)
- Manage tasks (Aisle Planner / Asana do that)
- Send invoices (HoneyBook / Stripe direct do that)

When something looks CRM-shaped, the question is whether the brain needs to consume it (yes - import, mine, learn) vs replicate it (no - that's CRM scope creep).

## Why "brain" is the right bet

1. **The signal is the moat.** Every venue has the same data sources (Knot, WeddingWire, Instagram, email, contracts). What differs is who wires them into a coherent identity graph and feeds that into AI drafts. Nobody else does this; everyone else builds CRM features.

2. **B2B SaaS pricing supports the brain shape.** A brain that surfaces "this couple is your lookalike of last year's $40K bookings" pays for itself at $299-799/mo. A CRM tool at the same price competes with HoneyBook's $9-66/mo and loses on feature breadth.

3. **CRMs are commodity, brains are scarce.** HoneyBook has 100+ engineers shipping CRM features. Bloom has one. The brain layer is uncontested.

4. **Brain compounds across venues.** A multi-venue customer's brain learns from every wedding at every venue. CRM doesn't compound — adding a tenth venue is just ten times the data, not a smarter system.

## Tests this positioning has to pass

- "Why don't you just use HoneyBook?" → "HoneyBook is the CRM we import from. Bloom is the brain that learns your voice and reconstructs your couple's identity from every signal."
- "How is this different from Aisle Planner?" → "Aisle Planner manages tasks. Bloom answers questions you didn't know to ask."
- "What if HoneyBook adds AI?" → "They add AI on their CRM data. Bloom learns from every source — their export plus 5+ others — and from your venue's specific voice. We're cross-source, they're single-source."
- "Why isn't this a Wedgewood internal tool?" → "Wedgewood needs the same brain across 50+ venues. Building it internally is a 2-year diversion from operating venues. Renting Bloom is faster + the brain learns from non-Wedgewood venues too."

## Implications for downstream strategy

- **Pricing:** the brain is worth $299-799/mo even at small venues because the alternative is hiring a part-time analyst. Don't undercut.
- **Sales motion:** sells on insights demonstrated, not on feature count. "Here's what the brain found in your last 12 months" beats "Here are 47 features."
- **Hiring:** ML/AI/data engineers > full-stack generalists. The team's bottleneck is brain quality, not surface area.
- **Acquisition story:** an acquirer pays for the brain (data graph + voice training + identity engine), not for the planning UI. UI is replaceable; the data network effects are not.
- **Roadmap:** every quarter's roadmap should make the brain demonstrably smarter. Quarter that ships only CRM-shaped features is a wasted quarter.

## Anti-positioning

When in doubt, ask "is this a brain feature or a CRM feature?" If CRM-shaped:
- Skip it
- OR import-only (Bloom consumes; HoneyBook keeps owning)
- OR ship it ONLY because it produces a signal the brain learns from

If you can't articulate the brain-feedback loop, it's CRM scope creep.
